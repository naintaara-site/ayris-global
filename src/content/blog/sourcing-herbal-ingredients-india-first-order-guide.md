Every successful long-term sourcing relationship with an Indian herbal ingredient supplier began with a first enquiry, a first sample, and a first order. That first step is also where most new buyers hesitate — uncertain about what to expect, what to ask, how much to order, how long it will take, and how to protect themselves in an unfamiliar cross-border trade relationship.

This guide removes that uncertainty. It walks through the practical process of sourcing herbal ingredients from India for the first time: from initial supplier contact through sample evaluation, first commercial order, documentation, payment, and shipment — in plain language, with realistic expectations, and with the detail that buyers actually need.

Whether you are a supplement brand placing your first bulk order of ashwagandha extract, a functional food producer sourcing moringa powder, a tea brand looking for tulsi and ginger, or a private label wellness company building your first India supply relationship — the process follows the same framework.

## Step 1: Define Your Requirements Before You Contact Anyone

The most common mistake new buyers make is approaching Indian suppliers with vague, exploratory enquiries before they have defined their own requirements. This produces generic responses, wastes time on both sides, and makes it difficult to compare suppliers meaningfully.

Before you send a single enquiry, define:

**Product specification.** What ingredient do you need? In what form — whole herb, powder, extract, essential oil? What grade — food grade, pharmaceutical grade, cosmetic grade? For extracts, what standardisation — what active constituent, at what percentage? For powders, what particle size? Having even an approximate specification signals to suppliers that you are a serious, qualified buyer.

**Quantity.** How much do you need for your first order? How much do you expect to need over 12 months? Indian suppliers use both initial order quantity and projected annual volume to assess whether a buyer relationship is worth investing in. A buyer who needs 25 kg for a first trial and expects to order 500 kg per year is a more attractive prospect than one who only communicates the trial quantity.

**Certification requirements.** Does your product or market require organic certification (NPOP/EU Organic/USDA NOP)? Halal or kosher? GMP-compliant manufacture? Specific testing standards (EU pesticide MRLs, US heavy metal limits, Japanese food additive standards)? Knowing your certification requirements before engaging suppliers saves considerable time and prevents the frustration of qualifying a supplier who cannot meet your market's documentation requirements.

**Timeline.** When do you need the first shipment? Working backwards from your production or launch schedule through shipment transit time and order lead time gives you the date by which a supplier needs to confirm and begin production.

## Step 2: Finding and Shortlisting Suppliers

India has thousands of herbal ingredient exporters ranging from sophisticated, internationally certified manufacturers to small informal traders. Navigating this landscape without prior India market knowledge is the central challenge for new buyers.

**Trade directories and B2B platforms** — IndiaMART, TradeIndia, Global Sources — provide supplier directories but require careful vetting. Many listings are brokers or aggregators rather than manufacturers, and claimed certifications cannot be assumed to be current or accurately scoped.

**APEDA's exporter database** (the Agricultural and Processed Food Products Export Development Authority) lists registered Indian exporters of herbal and agricultural products and provides a baseline credential check.

**Industry trade shows** — Vitafoods Europe (Geneva), Natural Products Expo West (USA), Fi Europe — regularly feature verified Indian herbal ingredient manufacturers and provide face-to-face supplier evaluation opportunities that significantly accelerate the qualification process.

**Specialist sourcing partners** like [Ayris Global](/for-buyers/) offer a more direct route. For buyers placing a first India order, working with a sourcing partner who has already done the supplier due diligence is often the most time- and risk-efficient approach.

Whichever route you use, shortlist three to five suppliers before requesting samples. This gives you a comparison basis and reduces the risk of over-committing to a single supplier before you have validated quality.

## Step 3: The Sample Request — What to Ask For and What to Expect

Requesting samples is the essential first step in any new supplier relationship. It is also where buyers can set the tone for the professional standard they expect.

**What to request in a sample enquiry:**

Send a structured written enquiry (email is standard) that includes your product specification, the quantity of sample you need, your intended application, your certification requirements, and a request for the following documentation alongside the sample:

- Product specification sheet (tech sheet) with typical analysis values
- Recent batch Certificate of Analysis (CoA) from an accredited third-party laboratory
- Copies of relevant certifications (organic, GMP, halal, FSSAI licence)
- Supplier profile or company credentials document

**Sample quantities and costs:** For herbal powders and extracts, a standard evaluation sample is typically 100g to 500g. Many established Indian suppliers provide evaluation samples free of charge (with courier cost either absorbed or charged to the buyer). Some charge a nominal sample fee, particularly for premium or standardised extracts. Paying a modest sample fee is often a positive signal — it indicates the supplier treats sample provision as a genuine evaluation investment rather than a marketing exercise.

**What a good sample response looks like:** A supplier who responds promptly (within 24–48 hours on business days), provides the documentation you requested alongside the physical sample, and answers your specification questions accurately is demonstrating the operational responsiveness and documentation competence you will need in a commercial relationship. A supplier who sends a sample without documentation, responds vaguely to specification questions, or takes more than a week to respond to an initial enquiry is showing you something important about how they will behave as a commercial partner.

**Evaluating the sample:** When the sample arrives, evaluate it against your specification: colour, aroma, texture, solubility (if relevant), and — ideally — independent third-party laboratory testing for identity, active constituent content, heavy metals, and pesticide residues. Do not rely solely on the supplier's CoA for a first evaluation. Having your own laboratory test the sample is standard practice for professional buyers and is the only way to verify the CoA's accuracy independently.

## Step 4: Minimum Order Quantities — What to Expect

Minimum order quantities (MOQs) for Indian herbal ingredients vary significantly by product category, supplier type, and whether the ingredient is a commodity or a speciality extract.

**For commodity herbal powders** (moringa, tulsi, ashwagandha root powder, ginger, turmeric) from established Indian producers, MOQs typically range from 25 kg to 100 kg per product for first orders, with lower per-unit pricing at higher volumes. These are negotiable, particularly for buyers who can demonstrate a credible annual volume projection.

**For standardised botanical extracts** (ashwagandha extract standardised to 5% withanolides, curcumin extract 95%, boswellia 65%, bacopa 20% bacosides), MOQs are typically 5 kg to 25 kg for first orders, reflecting the higher production cost and lower production run volumes. Some specialty extract manufacturers require minimum order values (e.g., USD 500–2,000) rather than weight-based MOQs.

**For essential oils**, MOQs are typically set by weight — 1 kg to 5 kg is common for commodity oils; higher for rare or slow-moving varieties.

**MOQ negotiation is normal and expected.** Indian suppliers understand that new buyers want to trial before committing to large volumes. A credible buyer who explains their business, their intended application, and their expected annual volume is in a reasonable position to negotiate a first-order MOQ below the standard minimum, particularly if they commit to a follow-up order at full volume if the sample and first order meet specification.

## Step 5: Lead Times — Realistic Expectations

Lead time from confirmed order to product ready for export typically ranges from two to six weeks for Indian herbal ingredients, depending on the product and supplier.

**In-stock commodity ingredients** (popular herbs and powders held in inventory by established exporters) can often be shipped within one to two weeks of order confirmation and payment.

**Made-to-order extracts and custom specifications** require production lead time. Allow three to five weeks from order confirmation to production completion for standardised extracts, longer for novel or bespoke formulations.

**Shipping transit time** from India to major import ports adds further time: approximately seven to fourteen days to Europe and the Middle East by sea freight, fourteen to twenty-one days to the USA or Australia by sea, and two to five days by air freight. Air freight is significantly more expensive but appropriate for urgent first samples, time-sensitive orders, or high-value, low-weight extract ingredients.

**Total timeline from first enquiry to received shipment for a first order** is realistically six to twelve weeks when accounting for sample evaluation, order confirmation, production, export documentation preparation, shipping, and customs clearance. Planning your sourcing timeline with this reality in mind prevents the stock-out situations that arise when buyers assume India supply chains operate at Western e-commerce speed.

## Step 6: Documentation — What You Will Receive with Your Shipment

A commercial shipment of herbal ingredients from India arrives with a standard set of export documents. Understanding these documents in advance prevents customs delays and ensures you can comply with import regulations at the destination port.

**Commercial Invoice** — states the product, quantity, unit price, total value, HS code, and terms of trade (Incoterms). Accuracy on the commercial invoice is critical; errors cause customs delays.

**Packing List** — details the number of cartons, gross and net weight, and dimensions of the shipment.

**Bill of Lading (sea)** or **Airway Bill (air)** — the transport document issued by the carrier, confirming shipment and serving as the document of title for sea freight.

**Certificate of Origin** — confirms the Indian origin of the goods. For buyers claiming preferential duty rates under free trade agreements (India-UAE CEPA, India-ASEAN FTA, etc.), a specific form of Certificate of Origin is required — for UAE and GCC imports under the India-UAE CEPA, this is typically a COO issued by an authorised Indian Chamber of Commerce. See our guide on the [India-UAE CEPA and herbal ingredient imports](/blog/india-uae-cepa-herbal-ingredients-gcc/) for specifics.

**Phytosanitary Certificate** — issued by NPPO India (the National Plant Protection Organisation), this certificate confirms that the plant-based material has been inspected and meets the phytosanitary requirements of the importing country. Required for most botanical material imports across the EU, USA, UAE, Japan, and other markets. Missing or incorrect phytosanitary certificates are a common cause of customs holds at destination ports.

**Certificate of Analysis (CoA)** — the batch-specific quality document confirming the product meets specification. Should be provided before shipment confirmation and accompany the shipment documentation.

For organic shipments, a **Transaction Certificate (TC)** issued by the NPOP-accredited certification body is required to maintain organic chain of custody.

For a complete overview of the documentation involved in herbal ingredient imports, our guide on [CoA, MSDS, and phytosanitary certificates for herbal imports](/blog/coa-msds-phytosanitary-certificates-herbal-imports/) covers each document in detail.

## Step 7: Payment Terms for First Orders

Payment terms for first orders with Indian herbal suppliers typically follow a conservative structure that protects the supplier's production investment with a buyer they have not yet traded with.

**100% advance payment (T/T in advance)** is the most common first-order payment structure for small to medium orders. The buyer pays the full invoice value before production begins or before shipment. This is standard in Indian herbal trade for new buyer relationships and should not be treated as a red flag — it reflects the reality that the supplier is investing in production and export preparation before receiving payment.

**50% advance, 50% against documents (T/T)** is a common structure for mid-size first orders, where half the payment confirms the order and the balance is paid when the supplier provides the shipment documents (Bill of Lading, CoA, phytosanitary certificate). This gives the buyer some leverage on documentation quality before final payment.

**Letter of Credit (LC)** is used for larger orders where the buyer requires a bank-intermediated payment mechanism that releases funds only when the supplier presents specified shipping documents. LC transactions add banking costs and administrative complexity and are typically reserved for orders above USD 10,000–20,000.

As a buyer relationship matures and trading history is established, payment terms typically become more favourable — moving toward net 30 or net 60 days from shipment, or a lower advance percentage with balance on delivery.
